Data Protection and Its Implications for Businesses (India & Kerala in Focus)

by Dhanya Menon | Aug 25, 2025 | Cyber Security | 0 comments

Data protection and cybercrime aren't just global buzzwords—they're relevant right here in India, and even more so in Kerala. With our ever growing digital ecosystem and increasing internet penetration, these issues have taken centre stage and not just background noise for both businesses and individuals. To understand this in more practical terms let’s break it down a bit: Data Protection and Its Implications for Businesses (India & Kerala in Focus):When we talk about data protection in India, the spotlight is firmly on the Digital Personal Data Protection Act (DPDP Act), 2023. Our answer to global standards like the GDPR So what does the Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 actually mean for businesses on the ground? Whether you're a startup founder, a small business owner, or part of an IT company in Technopark, this law is going to affect the way you handle customer data every single day. The DPDP Act isn’t just another piece of legislation—it’s got real teeth! it comes with serious consequences for non-compliance, making it a law that businesses can’t afford to ignore. Hefty Fines and Penalties: We're talking about penalties that can go up to ₹250 crore (around USD 30 million) for serious violations. That’s a massive risk, especially for micro, small, and medium enterprises (MSMEs) that may not have dedicated legal or compliance teams.

Data Fiduciary Responsibilities: If your business handles personal data, you’re what the Act calls a Data Fiduciary. That comes with a checklist of responsibilities, including: Lawful and Transparent Processing: You can’t just collect data without a clear, legitimate reason. People have a right to know what you’re doing with their information. Purpose Limitation: If you collect data to deliver a service, you can’t later use it for unrelated marketing or analytics without consent. Data Minimization: Only ask for what you really need. If someone’s signing up for a newsletter, you probably don’t need their full address and date of birth and last but definitely not the least and one of the most important is- Storage Limitation: Don’t hoard data forever. If it’s no longer needed, it should be safely deleted. If you’re handling personal data—and let’s keep it real, most businesses are these daysyou’ll need to rethink how you collect and manage it under the DPDP Act, 2023. Here are some key takeaways:

Always remember thatConsent is KeyBefore collecting someone’s data, you now need clear, informed, specific, and definite consent. No more hiding behind vague privacy policies or pre-checked boxes.This isn’t just legal nitpicking—it’s foundational. Whether you're a storeowner asking for phone numbers for a loyalty program etc or an app collecting user preferences, you need to be transparent and upfront.

Children’s Data requires Extra Caution- Handling data of individuals under 18? Herethe law gets stringent. Businesses must get verifiable parental consent, and also targeted advertising to children is a big no-no.This is especially important for Kerala’s growing edtech, entertainment, and e-commerce sectors. If your platform is kid-friendly, your data practices better be parent-approved.

  • Rights of the Data Principal: The Act empowers individualscalled Data Principalswith a set of strong rights, including:
  • The right to access their data, to correct inaccuracies, to erase data (yes that includes "right to be forgotten")and the right to raise grievances if something goes wrong.For businesses, this means having clear internal processes and systems to manage these requests quickly and efficiently.

Significant Data Fiduciaries (SDFs):More data meanmore responsibility!If you're a large business, or you deal with sensitive personal data for example- banks, hospitals, or major IT firmsyou might be classified as a Significant Data Fiduciary and the title comes with additional responsibilities: • Appointing a Data Protection Officer (DPO) based in India • Carrying out Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs) • Conducting regular, independent data audits In Kerala, with its booming healthcare sector, tech parks, and robust financial services, many organizations will fall into this category. Proactively gearing up for this is no longer optional, it's essential.

Breach Notification is MandatoryIf there's a data breach, you can’t sweep it under the rug. The Act mandates timely notifications to both the Data Protection Board and affected individuals. This makes having a solid incident response plan an operational must-have, not a luxury.One of the biggest shifts the DPDP Act, 2023 brings to the table is its strict stance on data breach notifications. If your business suffers a data breachwhether it’s customer email addresses being leaked or sensitive financial data falling into the wrong handsyou are legally required to inform both:The Data Protection Board of India (DPBI)And the affected individuals (i.e. the Data Principals). And here’s the catch: unlike some international laws like the GDPR, which only require notification if the breach is “likely to result in a risk or a possible vulnerability,” the DPDP Act mandates notification for all breachesno exceptions. It’s a tough standard to meet. So, whether you’re a tech startup in Kochi or an insurance provider in Thrissur, it’s time to get your breach response playbook in place. Quick detection, prompt reporting, and transparent communication are now part of your legal and ethical responsibility. While the DPDP Act is clearly the game changer now, it hasn’t entirely replaced the older framework—yet. I am talking about the IT Act and old rules.

Until the DPDP Act is fully rolled out and enforced across all sectors, the existing Information Technology Act, 2000 and its accompanying rules from 2011 (specifically, the Reasonable Security Practices and Procedures and Sensitive Personal Data or Information Rules) continue to be relevant.These older laws still provide important guidance on things like: What qualifies as sensitive personal data The kind of security measures businesses should have in place How consent should be obtained and documented So, for now, businesses need to juggle both: staying compliant with the IT Act while also preparing for full DPDP implementation. It may sound like a lot, but it’s ultimately about building a safer, more transparent digital ecosystemfor your customers, and for your business too. The Cost of Compliance: What It Means for Businesses in KeralaLet’s face itcomplying with the DPDP Act, 2023 isn’t just about ticking boxes. For many businesses, especially in Kerala, it’s also about managing real financial costs. MSMEs and the Compliance ChallengeKerala has a vibrant MSME ecosystem, spanning everything from traditional industries to modern digital services. But for these smaller players, getting data privacy-ready can be unnerving.Imagine updating IT systems, drafting new privacy policies, training staff on new data handling practices- it all adds up. For many MSMEs, this could mean diverting resources from day-to-day operations just to stay compliant. The Rising Need for Legal & Cybersecurity ExpertsAs the law gets more complex, businesses will increasingly need to lean on specialized consultants, whether it's for legal advice, drafting compliance documents, or conducting security audits. That means a growing demand (and cost) for lawyers and cybersecurity professionals who understand the nuances of the DPDP Act. Kerala's legal and tech services sectors may see a boom here but for individual businesses, this expertise won’t come cheap. Investing in Cybersecurity Is No Longer OptionalOne of the necessities under the DPDP Act is implementing reasonable security safeguards. This could mean deploying strong encryption standards, access controls to limit data exposure, real-time incident response plans and monitoring tools.For businesses in Kerala, especially those handling sensitive customer data, this translates to higher IT and cybersecurity investments. But let’s see the silver lining here: the cost of prevention is far less than the cost of a breach. Reputational Risks & The Trust FactorWhile legal consequences are serious, the real damage from a data breach often shows up in a less visible but equally critical area which is “Trust”. Public Awareness Is RisingIndia is still catching up when it comes to everyday awareness about data privacybut that’s changing fast. With the DPDP Act pushing the conversation into the mainstream, public scrutiny is only going to intensify.And in Kerala’s cohesive communities, where word spreads like wild fire, even a minor breach can spiral into a major trust issue. Businesses that fumble on privacy will be a herculean task to recover public confidence and trust. Sectors at Risk: Tourism & HealthcareKerala’s tourism and healthcare sectors handle some of the most sensitive personal data out there such as passport details, travel itineraries, or even patient health records. A slightest breach in these sectors doesn’t just mean legal trouble; it could also lead to colossal damage. It can trigger national headlines, disrupt tourist arrivals, overall economic growth and shake patient confidence in an industry built purely on care and trust. Operational Overhaul: With new laws come new ways of workingand for many businesses, this means rebuilding operations from the scratch when it comes to data handling. Data Mapping is Step One: From boutique hotels in Wayanad to hospitals in Ernakulam, businesses will now need to map out exactly what personal data they collect, where it's stored, and how it’s used. This isn’t just a technical auditit’s a mindset shift toward transparency. Consent Management: A New Must-Have: You’ll need to implement systems that allow you to obtain, record, and manage user consentclearly, and in a way that’s easy to update or withdraw. This might be unfamiliar territory for many businesses, and as challenging as it is, consent management is the need of the hour. Businesses must overpass this bridge at all cost. Cross-Border Data Transfers: Not a Free Pass: Unlike Europe’s GDPR, India’s DPDP Act is a bit more flexible on data transfers abroadbut that doesn’t mean it’s a free pass. Businesses with international tiesespecially in IT services or tourismmust still ensure they meet the Act’s core principles when sending data overseas. Training is CriticalYou can have the best policies in place, but if your team doesn’t understand them, they’re just paper. Educating employeesfrom front desk staff to backend developersis a critical, ongoing responsibility. Every person handling data is part of the privacy chain. How Compliance Can Boost Your Business:Let’s flip the script: what if all these changes could actually give your business a strategic advantage?In a world increasingly concerned about privacy, being known for data protection is atoken of trust. Kerala businesses or businesses anywhere else that embrace the DPDP Act early and thoroughly will stand outnot just locally, but to clients and tourists from countries with stronger privacy expectations giving them the competitive edge over others. This also creates an image of trust with the customers for their anticipatory response. Fuel for Innovation: With growing demand for privacy-by-design products, secure apps, and privacy-enhancing technologies (PETs), there is real opportunity here for Kerala’s tech startups. Building privacy into your product from day one isn’t just good complianceit’s also good business. Ignore data protection, and you’re not just risking a fine but also putting your reputation, customer trust, and long-term growth on the line. In a digital world, trust is currencyand without it, businesses don’t stand a chance!

2. Cybercrime: The Hidden Threat Lurking in Kerala’s Digital Growth Story

As Kerala rides the wave of digital transformation with smart governance, thriving IT parks, and a booming online tourism sector it’s also increasingly in the crosshairs of cybercriminals. While cybercrime is a national concern, Kerala’s high internet penetration, digitized services, and vibrant MSME ecosystem make it a uniquely vulnerable and valuable target. And the impacts? They’re more than just technical they’re financial, operational, reputational, and legal. Let’s unravel how cybercrime is redesigning the business risk landscape across the state. Direct Financial Hits: From Ransomware to Phishing- Cyberattacks don’t just target big corporations. In fact, many of Kerala’s small and medium businesses have found themselves victims of ransomware attacks, often forced to pay up or face total data loss. For a small enterprise, this can mean closing operations overnight. Phishing & Account Takeovers: Phishing scams, fake emails, cloned websites, deceptive phone calls are rambling, targeting both customers and employees. One wrong click can lead to corporate account takeovers, payment fraud, or much worse. Banking Fraud is on the Rise: With Kerala’s strong banking network (including co-operative banks and NBFCs), financial frauds like UPI scams, card cloning, and online banking breaches are a growing concern, putting both institutions and customers at risk. Intellectual Property Theft: For the state’s escalating startup scene and IT sector, cyber theft can be especially detrimental. A leak of source code, product designs, or business strategies can set a company back by years. Kerala has made big strides in digitizing public services, education, and healthcare. But these very strides make the state more susceptible. A single cyberattack can halt services across sectors, affecting thousands in real time.Many businesses here rely on interconnected digital vendors. That means a breach in one weak link can cause a domino effect across the supply chainespecially for industries like tourism, IT services, and healthcare. Its engaged civil society and active local media mean that cyber incidents rarely stay private. A hacked tourism site or a compromised hospital system can quickly become front-page news.Frequent cyber incidents can threaten broader public confidence in flagship initiatives like Digital India and Digital Kerala. The promise of convenience can start to feel like a liability when data isn’t safe. Given that the tourism industry thrives on international trust and online bookings, a single data breach or website defacement could daunt thousands of potential visitors.Cybercrime doesn’t just hurt your walletit can bring legal headaches too. Under the Information Technology Act, 2000, various forms of cyber fraud, hacking, and identity theft are punishable.CERT-In (India’s cybersecurity watchdog) mandates reporting significant cyber incidents within 6 hours, a tight timeline many are unprepared for.And for financial entities, RBI’s cybersecurity guidelines are non-negotiable. Ignoring them can lead to penalties and even license suspension. A new Cultural Shift: Cybersecurity is Everyone’s Job Now Kerala’s organizations are waking up to a new reality: cybersecurity isn’t just an IT concernit’s a macro issue.More companies are investing in firewalls, monitoring tools, and third-party auditsbut unfortunately there is a shortage of skilled cybersecurity professionals and that continues to be a major hurdle. The weakest link in most cyberattacks are us Humans. That’s why ongoing employee training and awareness programs are becoming essential, especially with the rise of social engineering attacks that trick employees into handing over credentials.Organizations are now realizing that preventing attacks is only half the battle. Building strong incident response plans, disaster recovery systems, and practicing regular simulations are important for long-term resilience. Why is Kerala unique Kerala’s Cyber Landscape is unique in its own way and here are some of the reasons why cybersecurity here has its own flavour:
Technopark &Infopark: These major IT hubs handle enormous data volumesand hold the potential to become national leaders in cybersecurity solutions.
Tourism: Handling sensitive financial and identity data from global tourists makes cybersecurity a frontline issue.
Healthcare: With rich medical data at stake, hospitals and clinics need airtight systemsgoing beyond even DPDP Act requirements.
MSMEs: The backbone of Kerala’s economy, these businesses are often the most underprepared.
Digital Governance: With high e-governance adoption, ensuring secure digital public infrastructure is critical.
Despite high digital usage, many small businesses and citizens still lack basic cybersecurity literacya gap that needs pressing attention.For businesses in Kerala, cybercrime isn’t just a background risk—it’s a clear and present danger. And steering this landscape means doing more than just ticking off checkboxes under the DPDP Act.It’s also about survival and compliance, building a culture of privacy, resilience, and digital responsibility, not just for legal safety, but for long-term business health and customer trust.
